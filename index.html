Finding the Lowest Point: An Introduction to Gradient Descent

Imagine you're standing on a mountainside in dense fog. You can only see a few feet in front of you, but your goal is to reach the bottom of the valley. How would you do it? A sensible strategy would be to feel the ground around you and take a step in the direction of the steepest descent. Keep repeating this process, and eventually, you'll reach the valley floor.

Continue

This "feel and step" approach is analogous to how Gradient Descent works. In Machine Learning, we often deal with complex problems where we need to find the "lowest point" of a function. This lowest point represents the optimal solution to our problem. This function we're trying to minimize is called the cost function, and it measures how "bad" our current solution is.

The Math Behind the Descent

Let's formalize this intuitive idea with a bit of math. Don't worry; we'll break it down step by step. The key to finding the steepest descent lies in the concept of a gradient. The gradient of a function at a particular point tells us the direction of the steepest increase. Since we want to go downhill, we'll move in the opposite direction of the gradient.

Continue

Think of a simple function like \(y = x^2\). Its derivative is \(\frac{dy}{dx} = 2x\). The derivative at a point \(x\) gives us the slope of the function at that point, which indicates the direction of the steepest ascent.

In Gradient Descent, we use this slope information to update our current guess. We take a step proportional to the negative of the gradient. The size of this step is controlled by a parameter called the learning rate (α), a small positive number.

Continue

The core update rule of Gradient Descent is:

\[ \beta_{new} = \beta_{old} - \alpha * \nabla C(\beta_{old}) \]

Let's break down this formula:

  • \(\beta_{old}\): Our current guess for the optimal solution. Think of this as your current position on the mountain.
  • \(\alpha\): The learning rate. This determines how big a step we take. A smaller learning rate means smaller steps, while a larger learning rate means bigger steps.
  • \(\nabla C(\beta_{old})\): The gradient of the cost function (C) at our current guess (\(\beta_{old}\)). This tells us the direction of the steepest increase in the cost function. The negative sign ensures we move in the direction of decreasing cost.
  • \(\beta_{new}\): Our updated guess after taking a step. This is your new position after taking a step downhill.

Stop and Think

If the learning rate (α) is too large, what could happen during the Gradient Descent process? What if it's too small?

If the learning rate is too large, the algorithm might overshoot the minimum and fail to converge. It's like taking overly large steps down the mountain and ending up further away from the bottom. If the learning rate is too small, the algorithm might take too long to converge or get stuck in a local minimum, similar to taking tiny steps and never quite reaching the valley floor.

Bringing it Together: An Example

Let's say our cost function is \(C(\beta) = \beta^2\), and our current guess is \(\beta_{old} = 3\). We'll use a learning rate of \(\alpha = 0.1\).

  1. Calculate the gradient: \(\nabla C(\beta_{old}) = 2\beta_{old} = 2 * 3 = 6\)
  2. Update the guess:

    \[ \beta_{new} = \beta_{old} - \alpha * \nabla C(\beta_{old}) \]

    \[ \beta_{new} = 3 - 0.1 * 6 \]

    \[ \beta_{new} = 2.4 \]

Our updated guess is now 2.4. We repeat this process until we reach a point where the updates become very small, indicating that we're close to the minimum.
